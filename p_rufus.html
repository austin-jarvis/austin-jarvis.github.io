Rufus Cage School Rehabilitation 2017 Houston, TX Client: TXRX

The roots of Mission Revival Style were conceived in the early settlements of the Spanish missionaries exploring California in the 18th century. These settlers devised a form of building that was reminiscent of the Spanish architecture they left behind but newly adapted for more simplistic construction techniques, rudimentary building materials, and a hot desert climate. The Rufus Cage school’s original brickwork, structural arches, smoothed stucco, timber roof, and high ceilings all tell a story of another time in architecture. But this is not a poor pairing with the simple cinder block and steel-truss construction of the warehouses adjacent to the Rufus Cage school. Each building reflects a unique era of pragmatic design and ad hoc construction.

Our vision for the Rufus Cage project is to create a layered site that links old to new for a holistic learning environment. TXRX Labs will adapt the site for the children’s maker-program which necessitates adaptations for learning, working, and display space - all while conforming with the requirements of the City’s Historic Preservation Manual. The landmark Mission building prevails as the most visible element and will serve as the primary public contact point. The entry level will act as a public gallery with classrooms on the second floor and a public bike repair shop below. The cafeteria building behind the school will be renovated into an auditorium for lectures and presentations. The warehouses on the Northern edge of the site will serve as a focused workspace for students and teachers. The primary new element will be a landscaped courtyard that ties these buildings together through three sculpted alcoves, creating an exterior room that brings coherence to the mixed styles and programs. Each building will have two entries: one street-facing entry allowing independent function; one courtyard-facing threshold that allows the three structures to act as one.
